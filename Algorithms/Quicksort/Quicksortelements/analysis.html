Analysis of Quick Sort Algorithm

Time Complexity of Quick Sort

Quick Sort is an efficient sorting algorithm that uses the divide-and-conquer approach. Its time complexity varies based on the choice of pivot:

  • Best Case: O(n log n) - When the pivot divides the array into two equal halves.
  • Average Case: O(n log n) - Generally occurs in random data.
  • Worst Case: O(n²) - Happens when the smallest or largest element is consistently chosen as the pivot.

Pivot Selection Techniques

The performance of Quick Sort heavily depends on how the pivot is chosen. Common strategies include:

  • First Element: Simple but can lead to poor performance on already sorted data.
  • Random Element: Helps in achieving average case time complexity consistently.
  • Median-of-Three: Selects the median of the first, middle, and last elements to improve performance.

Space Complexity of Quick Sort

The space complexity of Quick Sort is O(log n) due to the recursive stack space used for the divide-and-conquer approach. However, in the worst case, it can go up to O(n) for unbalanced partitions.

Comparison with Other Sorting Algorithms

Quick Sort is often compared with other sorting algorithms:

  • Merge Sort: Guarantees O(n log n) time but requires additional space. Quick Sort is generally faster in practice due to better cache performance.
  • Bubble Sort: Much slower with O(n²) time complexity, making Quick Sort far more efficient.
  • Insertion Sort: Performs well on small or nearly sorted datasets but has O(n²) complexity, while Quick Sort maintains O(n log n) efficiency.
